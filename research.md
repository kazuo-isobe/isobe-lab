# Research


Our group currently focuses on three main themes, combining fieldwork, experiments, and large-scale data analysis.

### Soil microbe–plant interactions sustaining ecosystem stability
We investigate how soil microbes—including beneficial symbionts, decomposers, and pathogens—interact with plants to sustain ecosystem stability. Our research examines these interactions across different plant species and evaluates how they respond to environmental change, including climate change. Ultimately, we aim to understand how plant–microbe interactions regulate ecosystem stability and resilience.

### Soil microbe–plant interactions promoting ecosystem succession
We study how interactions between soil microbes and plants drive ecosystem succession and soil development. Our research focuses on primitive ecosystems, such as desert soils, to identify how microbial processes and plant–microbe interactions promote primary succession. These insights contribute to strategies for preventing desertification and enhancing soil fertility.

### Phylogenetic framework for understanding microbial biogeography and environmental responses
We develop phylogenetic and trait-based frameworks to understand microbial biogeography and environmental responses. Specifically, we investigate whether interactions among soil microbes, plants, and environmental factors are context-dependent or follow consistent global patterns. By integrating microbial phylogenetic information with environmental data, we aim to predict microbial community responses and their consequences for ecosystem processes across ecosystems and climate gradients.
